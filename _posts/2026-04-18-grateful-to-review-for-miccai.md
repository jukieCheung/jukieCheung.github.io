I feel genuinely honored to have the opportunity to review for MICCAI this year. Since I started working on medical imaging and AI for healthcare, this is the first time I have been able to contribute, even in a small way, to a conference of this importance. For a junior researcher like me, that means a lot.

The four papers assigned to me were, in one way or another, connected to topics I had touched before—EEG, osteoarthritis, and statistical shape modeling. They were still very different from each other, which probably says something about my own research path so far: I am still early in my career, and my interests have not exactly followed a neat or linear trajectory.

To be honest, the way I read papers has changed a lot over the past few years. At PolyU, I have spent most of my time in a rehabilitation science environment. That experience made me much less impressed by technical novelty alone. I now care far more about whether a paper has real scientific meaning, whether the application scenario is seriously motivated, and whether the authors are actually trying to explain something rather than just report a better number.

Because of that, I have become quite picky. I am rarely persuaded by papers that stack technical modules, chase SOTA, and then leave the scientific or clinical side underexplained. MICCAI is of course a technical conference, and strong methods absolutely matter. But I do not think the community wants MICCAI to become a place where people simply recycle last year’s vision ideas, paste them onto a medical problem, and call it innovation.

Medical AI should not be reduced to benchmark chasing in a clinical costume.

That was probably my strongest feeling while reviewing these submissions. All four papers were technical to different degrees, but I found myself consistently drawn to work that started from a genuinely interesting question or offered a fresh way of looking at the problem. In particular, two papers stood out to me because they were not just trying to win on performance, they were trying to say something new. I appreciated that a lot, and I honestly hope to see more work like this in the field.

Reviewing these papers was a valuable experience. It also clarified, for myself, what kind of research I respect and what kind of research I want to do. Better metrics are nice, but they are not enough. A paper should also tell us why the problem matters, what we are really learning, and whether the work moves the field forward in a meaningful way.

I am grateful for the opportunity to review for MICCAI 2026, and I hope I can continue contributing to the community not only by building models, but also by asking harder questions about what is actually worth building.
